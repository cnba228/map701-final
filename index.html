Power and Authority Behind Cholera Mapping

Authored by: Caitlin Baer for New Maps Plus



In the early 1830’s cholera swiftly travelled throughout the world killing many in its path. Medical cartography is one of the most essential data visualization tools we have in the context of public health. Many argue that early cholera maps created a foundation to reinforce underling Western ideologies about disease, health, and illness (1). The notion that maps act to advance power relationships has been well speculated over the decades (2,3), and disease mapping is no novelty.

Lack of understanding for the disease paired with the rapidly-growing panic inspired a need for medical treaties and maps. Around this time, the growing output of disease maps was largely fueled by the debate between two camps of academics---the contagionists (those who believed cholera could be spread between people) and the anti-contagionists (those who believed that disease was incited by local, rotting organic matter rather than infected people)(4). Disease maps became an ideal medium for physicians to advocate their theories.

Scholars, like Schulten, highlighted the diverse approaches used to design maps based on which theory the creator supported. Amariah Brigham maps the spread of cholera from India and China to Europe and North America. In contrast, Meredith produced a map designed to demonstrate the non-contagious nature of cholera. His take included a plan designed to demonstrate cholera was caused by noxious air from decaying matter in the area.

Disease maps were often created to effect action (sanitation measures, policy, etc.) By creating a map in this way, the cartographer can establish power and ownership over the representation depicted. Through inclusion and exclusion, they demonstrate their perspective and actualize the rhetoric of the outbreak. As data visualization technologies have grown over the last few decades, significant implications for public health decision making have followed suit.
